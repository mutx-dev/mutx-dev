1. Sintesi esecutiva
La sintesi di tutto il materiale raccolto è netta: MUTX è tecnologicamente interessante, ma commercialmente troppo 
ampio per essere venduto oggi come prodotto enterprise standard. La mossa giusta è creare una wedge iniziale chiamata 
PicoMUTX: una forma più semplice, delimitata e commerciale del prodotto, pensata per entrare nelle aziende attraverso 
un problema preciso, non attraverso una promessa generica.

Il primo mercato più credibile è la provincia di Perugia, con focus su PMI manifatturiere, metalmeccaniche, impiantistiche 
e tecniche che vivono di email, documenti, preventivi, solleciti, ordini e passaggi manuali.

Il messaggio corretto non è 'portiamo agenti AI'. Il messaggio corretto è 'riduciamo attrito operativo su un processo 
specifico senza togliere controllo all’azienda'.

La sequenza giusta è: call di fit -> diagnosi pagata -> pilot 30/45 giorni -> layer operativo gestito -> build mirato -> 
upgrade MUTX solo dopo casi studio e fiducia.

Il primo obiettivo non è scalare. È chiudere 1-2 referenze locali eccellenti, con metriche semplici e testimonianza 
riutilizzabile.

Il prodotto core MUTX deve intanto maturare su cinque assi: orchestration hooks, permission system, lifecycle degli 
agenti, remote transport più pulito, skill system più vivo.
Decisione operativa
PicoMUTX deve essere trattato come un veicolo commerciale di ingresso. MUTX resta il motore, ma il cliente compra 
il caso d’uso, non l’engine.

MUTX / PicoMUTX — what the business plan still misses
Final internal memo. This version combines the GTM/business plan, the commercial playbook, the competitive report, the
technical blueprint, the operating-model debate, and the last round of objections.
Bottom line
The plan is mostly right on entry and still underdeveloped on transition. PicoMUTX is the correct commercial wedge.
MUTX-the-platform is the correct longer-term ambition. The unresolved issue is the bridge: when the company moves, what substrate
must exist, and who funds the gap.
1. What the plan gets right
The plan is correct that MUTX should not be sold today as a broad enterprise platform. The right first product is PicoMUTX: one
narrow workflow, one owner, one KPI, one bounded pilot, then expansion only after proof.
Its sequencing is strong: fit call → paid diagnosis → 30/45-day pilot → managed layer → targeted build work → broader
upgrade later. That protects scope, keeps the first sale concrete, and matches how Perugia SMEs actually buy: they want less
chaos in quotes, follow-up, procurement, and document-heavy backoffice flows, not a lecture about agents.
2. What the debate exposed
The real hole is not GTM. It is company design. There are effectively two businesses inside one story, and they do not share the
same economics, roadmap, or buyer.
Business

The current plan talks clearly about Business 1. The competitive report talks clearly about Business 2. What is still missing is a

Financial bridge — say it plainly
Using the latest working model assumptions from the debate, PicoMUTX appears to generate roughly ~€230k over 18 months with a
peak cash trough around ~€8k before a serious platform push. By contrast, even a minimal platform P0 build — OTel spine, audit, auth,
guardrails, one real adapter — likely means 2 senior engineers for 3–6 months, or roughly €50k–150k of extra burn. So the wedge
business does not comfortably self-fund the platform early unless the platform build waits, outside capital arrives, or Mario builds at
founder pace with AI coding leverage and accepts slower timing.
That is why “pilots will fund product” is directionally nice but still incomplete. It does not yet prove that wedge cash flow can carry a
real platform build on competitive timelines.
There is a second unresolved issue: the team is not fully aligned on which business it is building. The plan is wedge-first. The
competitive report is platform-first in product logic. Recent discussion introduced a third idea — a cheap self-serve PLG SaaS sold
through social channels. That is not a packaging tweak. That is a third business model. Until that is killed or chosen, messaging,
hiring, roadmap, and capital allocation will keep fighting each other.
3. What the competitive report gets right
The competitive report is right that MUTX is not a platform yet. It is a control-plane skeleton with one real differentiator —
governance — but without the boring substrate serious buyers will eventually require: traceability, audit, identity, approvals,
framework integrations, and durable operations.
Its sequencing is also right: OTel/event spine → audit/replay → auth → guardrails → first real adapter. That order matters
because everything else is fake progress if the system cannot tell you what happened, who did it, whether it was allowed, and how
to prove it later.
Where the report overreaches is timing. The 90-day roadmap reads like a real product organization. Mario is not a real product
organization. The correct takeaway is: use the report to rank what compounds, then cut execution down to what one
founder-led team can actually ship.
Practical product conclusion
If PicoMUTX is the commercial wedge, then the platform work running in parallel should be limited to compounding substrate only:
event model, auditability, permissions/auth hardening, approval controls, and observability hooks. Not five polished adapters. Not
protocol theatre. Not a fake enterprise dashboard.

4. The margin story hiding underneath
The technical blueprint matters here. It assumes a multi-tenant control plane plus per-customer environments, with optional BYOK
for enterprise tiers and a DigitalOcean-based deployment cost anchor. That implies a future software business with relatively light
variable COGS versus value, especially when customers carry their own model keys or security requirements.
That does not make today’s business a SaaS company. But it does show that there is a real software-margin story hiding under a
services-led wedge. Good. The mistake would be to confuse “good future software margins” with “we already have a self-serve
SaaS motion.”
5. The external force that makes all of this more urgent
The strongest why-now is regulation plus buyer anxiety. The competitive work is right to stress the EU AI Act timing: as the
obligations landing on August 2, 2026 get closer, governance, auditability, identity, and proof stop looking optional and start looking
like entry tickets. That helps both businesses: PicoMUTX can increasingly sell bounded automation with control, and the platform
story gets a stronger enterprise rationale.
That should shape priority: be louder about governance and proof, but not louder about platform ambition. Sell operational control
now; earn the right to sell the control plane later.
